\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}
Human Activity Recognition (HAR) using mobile device sensors has emerged as a cornerstone of context-aware computing, enabling advanced applications ranging from autonomous fitness tracking to geriatric care monitoring and predictive health modeling. This report details the comprehensive design, implementation, mathematical foundation, and rigorous evaluation of \textbf{five} distinct deep learning architectures applied to the UCI Human Activity Recognition dataset. We evaluate a Base Artificial Neural Network (MLP) as our naive baseline, a 1D Convolutional Neural Network (1D-CNN) for spatial feature extraction, a Vanilla Recurrent Neural Network (Simple RNN) for basic temporal modeling, a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network for advanced temporal dynamic modeling with gating mechanisms, and a Hybrid CNN-LSTM for dual spatial-temporal processing. Through exhaustive hyperparameter tuning and performance benchmarking, our findings demonstrate that the Hybrid CNN-LSTM architecture achieves the best classification accuracy of 95.88\%, successfully combining spatial feature extraction with temporal sequence modeling. A particularly illuminating finding was the direct comparison between Simple RNN (85.30\%) and LSTM (91.20\%), empirically validating how gating mechanisms solve the vanishing gradient problem.

\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Background and Motivation}
The ubiquity of modern smartphones equipped with highly sensitive, multi-axis micro-electro-mechanical systems (MEMS)---specifically accelerometers, gyroscopes, and magnetometers---has provided researchers with an unprecedented opportunity to analyze human kinetics continuously and unobtrusively in real-world environments. Human Activity Recognition (HAR) systems aim to interpret these raw, multidimensional, and highly noisy time-series signals to classify the physical state of the user into discrete categorizations, such as walking, sitting, laying down, or ascending stairs. The primary motivation behind optimizing and perfecting HAR models lies in their vast, transformative applicability. In the realm of healthcare, highly accurate HAR models can passively monitor rehabilitation patients, detect anomalies in the gait of elderly individuals indicative of falls or neurological decay (e.g., Parkinson's disease), and encourage active lifestyles through ambient assisted living systems.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
Despite the massive availability of raw sensor telemetry, accurately mapping high-dimensional, highly chaotic time-series vectors to discrete human activities remains a mathematically complex challenge. Sensor data is inherently plagued by Gaussian noise, gravitational interference, and inter-subject variability. Traditional machine learning models rely heavily on manual feature engineering, which requires immense domain expertise and is often non-generalizable across different devices. While deep learning models circumvent this by autonomously extracting abstract features, standard sequential models like MLPs and standard RNNs often struggle to dynamically weigh the importance of disparate sensor axes across long temporal sequences without suffering from severe information decay. This project aims to definitively address this by benchmarking five established deep learning pipelines, progressively increasing in architectural sophistication.

\section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Traditional Machine Learning in HAR}
Early approaches to HAR were heavily dominated by traditional statistical machine learning algorithms, including Support Vector Machines (SVMs), Random Forests, k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN), and Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). While computationally inexpensive and moderately effective, these models were fundamentally bottlenecked by the necessity for heuristic-driven feature extraction. Researchers were forced to manually calculate Fourier transforms, signal entropy, zero-crossing rates, and autoregressive coefficients to feed these models.

\subsection{The Shift to Deep Learning Architectures}
With the advent of deep learning and GPU acceleration, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) were adapted from the image processing domain for 1D time-series data. 1D-CNNs proved exceptionally capable of capturing local dependencies and spatial invariances within the sensor signals by dragging trainable convolutional filters across the time axis. Concurrently, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) were employed to model the temporal dynamics inherent in human movement. However, Vanilla RNNs suffered from the vanishing gradient problem, leading to the development of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks with explicit gating mechanisms.

\subsection{Hybrid Architectures}
Recent literature has extensively explored hybridizing CNN and LSTM architectures (CNN-LSTM networks) to leverage both the abstract spatial feature extraction of convolutions and the sequence memory of LSTMs. These hybrid models first use convolutional layers to extract high-level spatial features from the raw signals, then pass these features through LSTM layers to capture temporal dependencies across the extracted feature maps. This two-stage approach has shown consistent improvements over using either architecture in isolation.

\section{Detailed Methodology, Dataset, and Preprocessing}
\subsection{Hardware and Sensor Specifications}
The models were trained and evaluated using the highly respected benchmark \textbf{UCI Human Activity Recognition Using Smartphones Dataset}. The dataset was collected via a rigorous experiment involving 30 volunteers within an age bracket of 19 to 48 years. Each participant was instructed to perform six standard daily activities: WALKING, WALKING\_UPSTAIRS, WALKING\_DOWNSTAIRS, SITTING, STANDING, and LAYING. The embedded tri-axial accelerometer and tri-axial gyroscope captured linear acceleration and angular velocity at a constant, high-fidelity sampling rate of \textbf{50Hz}.

\subsection{Signal Filtering and Sliding Window Segmentation}
Raw sensor signals are heavily corrupted by sensor noise and gravitational forces. To clean the data, the sensor acceleration signals were passed through a median filter and a 3rd order low-pass Butterworth filter with a corner frequency of 20Hz. 

Because continuous data streams cannot be fed directly into standard classifiers, the continuous 50Hz signals were strictly segmented into fixed-width sliding windows of \textbf{2.56 seconds}. At 50Hz, this equates to exactly \textbf{128 readings per window}. To preserve transition contexts between movements, a 50\% overlap (64 readings) was enforced between consecutive windows.

\subsection{Comprehensive Feature Extraction}
From each 128-reading sliding window, a massive vector of \textbf{561 engineered features} was calculated to capture every possible nuance of the movement. The calculated variables include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Statistical Metrics:} Mean ($\mu$), Standard Deviation ($\sigma$), Median Absolute Deviation (MAD), Max, Min, and Signal Magnitude Area (SMA).
    \item \textbf{Energy and Entropy:} Signal energy (sum of squares divided by window length), Interquartile Range (IQR), and Signal Entropy.
    \item \textbf{Autoregression \& Correlation:} Autoregressive coefficients to model the signal, and correlation coefficients between the X, Y, and Z axes.
    \item \textbf{Jerk Signals:} The body linear acceleration and angular velocity were mathematically derived in time to obtain "Jerk" signals.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Scaling and Normalization}
We utilized a rigorous \texttt{StandardScaler} implementation to independently center each of the 561 features around a mean of zero with a standard deviation of one, applying the standard z-score normalization formula: $ z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} $. Crucially, to prevent data leakage from the future, the scaler was fitted \textit{strictly} on the training subset and subsequently applied to transform both the training and testing sets.

\subsection{Dataset Partitioning}
The dataset of 10,299 total sliding windows was randomly partitioned into two sets, where 70\% of the volunteers were selected for generating the training data and 30\% the test data.

\section{Proposed Deep Learning Architectures}
To provide an exhaustive comparative analysis, five unique neural network architectures were mathematically designed and developed using PyTorch. 

\subsection{Model 1: Base Artificial Neural Network (MLP)}
The multi-layer perceptron acts as our foundational baseline. It relies entirely on dense, fully connected linear transformations to map the 561-dimensional feature vector to the 6 output classes. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layer 1}: Linear Dense (561 $\rightarrow$ 256) $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ Dropout(0.3)
    \item \textbf{Layer 2}: Linear Dense (256 $\rightarrow$ 128) $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ Dropout(0.3)
    \item \textbf{Layer 3}: Linear Dense (128 $\rightarrow$ 6 classes)
\end{itemize}
The forward pass computes: $ \hat{y} = W_3 \cdot \text{ReLU}(W_2 \cdot \text{ReLU}(W_1 x + b_1) + b_2) + b_3 $

\subsection{Model 2: 1D Convolutional Neural Network (1D-CNN)}
To explicitly exploit local structural correlations within the sequential feature vector, the CNN applies 1-dimensional filters.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Block 1}: Conv1D (in\_channels: 1, out\_channels: 64, kernel\_size: 3) $\rightarrow$ ReLU
    \item \textbf{Block 2}: Conv1D (in\_channels: 64, out\_channels: 128, kernel\_size: 3) $\rightarrow$ ReLU
    \item \textbf{Pooling \& Classifier}: MaxPool1D $\rightarrow$ Linear Dense (128 * 280 $\rightarrow$ 6 classes)
\end{itemize}
The convolution operation is defined as:
\[ y[n] = \sum_{k=0}^{K-1} w[k] \cdot x[n+k] + b \]
where $K$ is the kernel size, $w$ are the learned filter weights, and $b$ is the bias.

\subsection{Model 3: Vanilla Recurrent Neural Network (Simple RNN)}
The Simple RNN is the most basic form of a recurrent architecture. It processes the 561 features as a sequence, maintaining a hidden state that is updated at each time step. However, it uses only a single \texttt{tanh} nonlinearity and has no gating mechanism, making it susceptible to the \textbf{vanishing gradient problem} on long sequences.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Recurrent Block}: RNN (input\_size: 1, hidden\_size: 64, nonlinearity: tanh, batch\_first: True)
    \item \textbf{Classifier}: Linear Dense (64 $\rightarrow$ 6 classes)
\end{itemize}
The hidden state update rule at each step $t$ is:
\[ h_t = \tanh(W_{ih} x_t + b_{ih} + W_{hh} h_{t-1} + b_{hh}) \]
where $x_t$ is the input at step $t$, $h_{t-1}$ is the previous hidden state, and $W_{ih}$, $W_{hh}$ are the input-to-hidden and hidden-to-hidden weight matrices respectively.

\textbf{Key Limitation:} During backpropagation through time (BPTT), gradients are multiplied by $W_{hh}$ at each step. Over 561 steps, if the eigenvalues of $W_{hh}$ are less than 1, gradients shrink exponentially toward zero (vanishing gradient), causing the network to ``forget'' early features entirely.

\subsection{Model 4: Long Short-Term Memory Network (LSTM)}
The LSTM is a direct evolution of the Simple RNN, specifically engineered to overcome the vanishing gradient problem via three explicit gating mechanisms and a dedicated cell state $c_t$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal Block}: LSTM (input\_size: 1, hidden\_size: 64, batch\_first: True)
    \item \textbf{Classifier}: Linear Dense (64 $\rightarrow$ 6 classes)
\end{itemize}
The LSTM computes four operations at each time step:
\begin{align}
    f_t &= \sigma(W_f \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f) & \text{(Forget Gate)} \\
    i_t &= \sigma(W_i \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i) & \text{(Input Gate)} \\
    \tilde{c}_t &= \tanh(W_c \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_c) & \text{(Candidate Memory)} \\
    c_t &= f_t \odot c_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{c}_t & \text{(Cell State Update)} \\
    o_t &= \sigma(W_o \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o) & \text{(Output Gate)} \\
    h_t &= o_t \odot \tanh(c_t) & \text{(Hidden State)}
\end{align}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function and $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication.

\textbf{Key Advantage over Simple RNN:} The cell state $c_t$ acts as a ``highway'' for gradients. The forget gate $f_t$ can be set close to 1, allowing gradients to flow backward through hundreds of time steps without decay. This enables the LSTM to remember important early features (e.g., initial acceleration patterns) even after processing all 561 steps.

\subsection{Model 5: Hybrid CNN-LSTM Architecture}
The Hybrid CNN-LSTM architecture combines the strengths of both convolutional and recurrent networks. The Conv1D layer acts as an initial sophisticated spatial feature extractor, reducing noise and capturing local patterns. This spatially refined tensor is then fed sequentially into the LSTM layer, which models temporal dependencies across the extracted feature maps.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spatial Stage}: Conv1D (in\_channels: 1, out\_channels: 64, kernel\_size: 3) $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ MaxPool1D(kernel\_size: 2)
    \item \textbf{Temporal Stage}: LSTM (input\_size: 64, hidden\_size: 128, batch\_first: True)
    \item \textbf{Classification Stage}: Linear Dense (128 $\rightarrow$ 6 classes)
\end{itemize}

The data flow through the hybrid architecture is:
\[ x \xrightarrow{\text{Conv1D}} \text{ReLU} \xrightarrow{\text{MaxPool}} x' \xrightarrow{\text{LSTM}} h_T \xrightarrow{\text{Linear}} \hat{y} \]

\textbf{Why this works:} The CNN first extracts high-level spatial features (e.g., detecting that body acceleration and gravity are both elevated), reducing the 561-dimensional input into a compressed, noise-free representation. The LSTM then processes these refined features sequentially, capturing any remaining temporal structure. This two-stage pipeline achieves the best of both worlds: spatial pattern recognition and sequential memory.

\section{Hardware and Software Implementation Details}
The entire computational pipeline, from data ingestion to model inference, was implemented using \textbf{PyTorch (v2.x)}. To ensure rapid matrix multiplication and backpropagation, all models were shifted to device memory and executed on hardware optimized with NVIDIA CUDA acceleration.

The training loop was rigidly standardized across all five distinct architectures to guarantee a fair and unbiased comparative environment. Key hyperparameters included:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Optimizer:} \textbf{AdamW} optimizer. AdamW decouples weight decay from the gradient update step, which has been mathematically proven to achieve superior generalization.
    \item \textbf{Learning Rate:} Fixed at $0.001$.
    \item \textbf{Weight Decay (L2 Penalty):} Set to $1e-4$.
    \item \textbf{Objective Function:} PyTorch's native \texttt{CrossEntropyLoss()}.
    \item \textbf{Epochs and Batching:} Training was conducted over \textbf{50 epochs} with mini-batches of size \textbf{64}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion and Future Scope}
This project successfully and rigorously explored the highly complex domain of Human Activity Recognition by benchmarking five distinct architectures: Multi-Layer Perceptrons, 1D-CNNs, Simple (Vanilla) RNNs, LSTMs, and Hybrid CNN-LSTMs. A particularly illuminating finding was the direct comparison between the Simple RNN (85.30\%) and the LSTM (91.20\%), which empirically validated how gating mechanisms and the cell state highway solve the vanishing gradient problem, yielding a substantial 5.9 percentage point improvement on the same sequential data.

The Hybrid CNN-LSTM architecture achieved the highest accuracy of 95.88\%, demonstrating that combining spatial feature extraction (via 1D convolution) with temporal sequence modeling (via LSTM) yields superior classification performance compared to using either approach in isolation. The CNN stage effectively denoises and compresses the raw 561-dimensional feature space, while the LSTM stage captures sequential dependencies across the refined feature maps.

\textbf{Future Work}: Future iterations of this project will explore:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Attention Mechanisms:} Integrating self-attention layers (Transformer-based architectures) to enable dynamic, input-dependent feature weighting for further accuracy improvements.
    \item \textbf{Raw Signal Processing:} Adapting the architectures to process raw, un-windowed 50Hz time-series data directly, bypassing the heuristic feature extraction phase entirely.
    \item \textbf{Edge Deployment:} Optimizing model architectures via quantization and pruning for deployment on low-resource edge devices (smartwatches, IoT sensors) for real-time inference.
\end{itemize}
